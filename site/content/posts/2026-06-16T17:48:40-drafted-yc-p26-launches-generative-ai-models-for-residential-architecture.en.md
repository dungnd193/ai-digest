# Drafted Brings Generative AI to the House You Haven't Built Yet

Generative models have already eaten the easy parts of design work — moodboards, marketing copy, throwaway concept art. The harder targets are the workflows where output has to satisfy hard constraints, survive professional scrutiny, and eventually become a real, physical thing. Residential architecture is squarely in that second category, which makes Drafted, a YC P26 startup, an interesting case study in pushing generative AI into a high-friction, high-cost professional pipeline.

## What it does

Drafted's models generate complete residential floor plans along with matching exterior elevations. The notable part isn't just that it produces a plan — it's that it produces it in seconds, from structured constraints rather than free-form prompting. Users feed in inputs like:

- Total square footage
- Footprint shape
- Lot boundaries
- Room placement

From those constraints, the system returns a coherent design. From there, the workflow opens up into the kind of iteration loop you'd expect from a serious design tool rather than a one-shot generator:

- Iterate in **2D and 3D**
- **Furnish interiors**
- **Test materials**
- **Export to CAD and PDF**

That export step matters more than it might first appear. A generated image is a dead end for a builder; a CAD/PDF export is a handoff. It's the difference between a tool that produces inspiration and a tool that produces artifacts the rest of the construction pipeline can actually consume.

## Why the constraint-first framing is the interesting bit

Most generative design demos start from a text prompt and dazzle you with variety. Architecture doesn't work that way. A house plan is only useful if it fits the lot, hits the square footage, and arranges rooms the way the owner needs them. Those aren't stylistic preferences you nudge after the fact — they're the problem definition.

By taking square footage, footprint shape, lot boundaries, and room placement as first-class structured inputs, Drafted is treating generation as constrained search rather than open-ended synthesis. That's the right framing for a domain where the output has to be *valid*, not just *plausible*. It also explains why the iteration story — 2D/3D, furnishings, materials — sits on top of generation rather than replacing it. The model gets you a feasible starting point fast; the human steers it toward the specific house they want.

## The wedge: a $10,000–$50,000, months-long process

The economic target here is explicit. Custom home design traditionally runs **$10,000 to $50,000** and takes **months**. That's the friction Drafted is aiming at, and it's the kind of friction that makes generative AI a genuinely good fit rather than a novelty:

- **High cost** means there's real money to disrupt, and real willingness to pay for an alternative.
- **Long timelines** mean the "in seconds" pitch isn't a marginal speedup — it's a different category of experience.
- **Expert gatekeeping** means a large pool of people are priced or timelined out of the process entirely, representing latent demand rather than just substitution.

That last point is worth dwelling on. When you collapse a months-long, five-figure process into something that runs in seconds, you don't just win existing customers away from incumbents — you surface a population that never seriously engaged with custom design because the cost and time made it irrational to even start.

## The demand signal

The early traction backs up that latent-demand thesis. In its first month, **over 120,000 people generated more than 325,000 home designs**. Roughly three designs per person, on average, is a telling ratio: it's not the signature of people generating one plan and bouncing. It's the signature of iteration — people coming in, generating, adjusting constraints, and generating again. That's exactly the behavior you'd want to see from a tool whose entire premise is making iteration cheap.

A month is a short window, and early-curiosity traffic is not the same as durable usage or revenue. But the volume is large enough, and the per-user repeat rate high enough, to read as real engagement rather than a launch-day spike.

## Why this launch is worth noticing

Step back from the specifics and Drafted is a clean example of a pattern that's becoming the more interesting frontier for generative AI: **vertical applications aimed at high-friction, high-cost professional workflows.** The general-purpose models grab headlines, but the durable value increasingly looks like it comes from wrapping generation in the constraints, file formats, and iteration loops of a specific domain — so the output isn't just impressive, it's *usable* by the people downstream.

Architecture is a demanding test of that thesis. The constraints are non-negotiable, the outputs have to export into real toolchains, and the buyers are weighing the result against established professional alternatives. If generative AI can produce a feasible, iterable, exportable home design in seconds against a process that normally costs tens of thousands of dollars and runs for months, that's a meaningful proof point for the broader bet — that the next wave of value comes from pointing these models at the expensive, slow, expert-gated workflows people have long assumed were safe from automation.
