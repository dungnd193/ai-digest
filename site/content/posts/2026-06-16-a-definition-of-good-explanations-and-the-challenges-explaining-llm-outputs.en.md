Ask yourself: when an AI system hands you an explanation, how do you know whether it's any good? Not whether it sounds fluent or confident—whether it actually *explains*. That deceptively simple question turns out to have no settled answer, and a recent paper argues the field has been treating it as a property of the model when it's really a property of the conversation.

## The problem with "explainability" as a noun

We tend to talk about explainability as if it were a dial on the model: turn it up, and outputs become more interpretable. The framing this work proposes pushes back hard on that intuition. An explanation, it argues, is not a fixed attribute that an output either has or lacks. It is **audience-relative**—its quality depends on who is receiving it and what they already believe.

This reframing matters because most of our tooling implicitly assumes the opposite. Attribution maps, saliency scores, and feature attributions are computed once and presented to everyone, as though a single artifact could serve a domain expert, a regulator, and an end user equally well. If explanation quality is relative to the interlocutor, then a "good explanation" detached from its audience is a category error—like asking whether a sentence is a good answer without knowing the question.

## Two ingredients: counterfactuals and priors

The framework grounds a good explanation in two things working together.

The first is **counterfactuals**. An explanation earns its keep by telling you what would have changed the outcome—how the result would differ under alternative conditions. This is a familiar move in the interpretability literature, and for good reason: counterfactual structure is what separates an explanation from a mere description. Saying "the model output X" describes; saying "had this been different, the output would have been Y" explains.

The second ingredient is the one that does the reframing work: **the interlocutor's prior beliefs**. The same counterfactual can be illuminating to one person and useless to another, depending on what they already hold to be true. An explanation's job is to move the listener from where their beliefs currently sit, and you cannot measure that movement without knowing the starting point.

Put together, the proposal is something like this: a good explanation is one whose counterfactual content productively engages the specific priors of the person it's addressed to. Quality lives in the gap between what they believed before and what they believe after—not in the artifact alone.

```
explanation quality
   = f( counterfactual content , interlocutor's prior beliefs )

   not
   = g( model output )
```

That small notational shift—from a function of the output to a function of output *and* audience—is the conceptual core of the contribution.

## Why LLMs make this acutely hard

The framework is general, but the paper is pointed at a specific and uncomfortable case: explaining the outputs of large language models. Several things conspire to make this harder than explaining a classifier's decision.

- **There's no obvious counterfactual handle.** With a tabular model you can perturb a feature and watch the prediction move. With an LLM producing open-ended text, what is the unit you vary, and what is the outcome you measure the variation against? The counterfactual machinery that grounds the definition is harder to operationalize when both input and output are unstructured language.
- **The interlocutor is unknown and plural.** LLMs are deployed to enormous, heterogeneous audiences. If explanation quality depends on prior beliefs, an explanation tuned to no one in particular is tuned to no one at all.
- **The explanation itself is generated by the same kind of system.** When the model can produce a fluent, plausible-sounding rationale on demand, surface persuasiveness can masquerade as genuine explanatory quality. A definition anchored in real belief change—rather than in how convincing the text reads—is a useful corrective here, because it refuses to grade an explanation on eloquence.

The thread connecting these is that the LLM setting strips away the conveniences that let us *pretend* explanation was a model property. It forces the audience back into the picture.

## What this is, and what it isn't

It's worth being honest about the kind of contribution this is. The work's value is **conceptual clarity**—it sharpens what we mean when we call an explanation good, and it gives the interpretability and XAI literature a more defensible foundation than "looks interpretable to me." That's real, but it is not a new method, a benchmark, or a tool you can `pip install` tomorrow.

The paper's own near-term influence is, by its framing, largely academic. The likelier path to practical impact runs through standards: if explainability requirements ever get written into regulation or industry norms, a rigorous, audience-relative definition is exactly the kind of groundwork those standards would need to rest on. A definition that survives scrutiny now is what keeps future rules from being built on the shaky premise that explanation is a property you can certify once and ship to everyone.

## The takeaway for practitioners

If you build or evaluate explanation systems, the actionable shift is to stop asking "is this explanation good?" and start asking "**good for whom, against what prior?**"

That reframing has teeth. It suggests that evaluating an explanation without modeling its audience is incomplete by construction. It suggests that counterfactual content is necessary but not sufficient—the same counterfactual has to land against a specific set of beliefs to do any work. And it suggests that for LLMs in particular, where the audience is vast and the output is fluent enough to fake its own justification, we should be most suspicious of explanations that look good to everyone, because an explanation calibrated to everyone is calibrated to no one.

None of this makes explaining LLM outputs easier. But naming the difficulty precisely—locating it in the relationship between counterfactuals and priors rather than in some missing model feature—is the first move toward addressing it honestly. You can't build good explanations until you can say what makes one good, and saying *that* is harder, and more relative, than the field's tooling has so far admitted.
